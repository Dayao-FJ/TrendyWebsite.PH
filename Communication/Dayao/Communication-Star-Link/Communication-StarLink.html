What is Starlink?

Starlink is a satellite-based internet system developed by SpaceX, the private space company founded by Elon Musk. Its goal is to provide high-speed, low-latency internet across the globe, especially in remote and underserved areas where traditional internet infrastructure is unavailable or weak.

These satellites connect with ground stations and user terminals (dishes) to transmit internet data. Because they orbit much closer to Earth than traditional satellites, they provide faster speeds and lower latency. Starlink also uses laser links between satellites for direct, high-speed data routing in space.

How Does It Work?

Network Evolution from 1G to 6G

Starlink operates through a constellation of thousands of small satellites orbiting in low Earth orbit (LEO). These satellites communicate with ground terminals (user dishes) and ground stations to deliver internet. Unlike traditional satellite internet, which has high latency due to long distances, Starlink’s low orbit enables faster data transmission and lower delay. The system also uses laser inter-satellite links for quicker and more direct routing of data between satellites.

When Is It Implemented?

Starlink began development in 2015, with the first prototype satellites launched in 2018. The first large batch of operational satellites was launched in May 2019, marking the start of its global deployment. In October 2020, Starlink opened public beta testing, known as "Better Than Nothing Beta," primarily in parts of the U.S., Canada, and the UK. Since then, coverage and performance have rapidly expanded.

Where Is It Already Implemented?

Starlink is implemented to close the global digital divide by providing internet access to regions where cable or fiber connections are impractical. It also supports emergency communications, military operations, mobile users, and IoT infrastructure. The network helps improve connectivity, education, business, and disaster response in areas with limited technological access.
